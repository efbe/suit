Suit

Personal experiment

Based on the ups & downs of Snorky (see below) and Ustream's huge amount of redundant static code I started to play with my very own open source module based front end framework. My problem with the existing ones like Bootstrap and Zurb is that they are barely customizable without duplicating the codebase because you don't extend but mostly overwrite rules making the amount of required static asset quite large for mobile platforms and poor internet connection.

In Suit's SASS based setup there's a structural and a design layer for each and every module. The core part resets, sets up and positions the elements while the theme part handles the design related CSS rules from gutters to block heights and from colors to border sizes declared in a config file. This way you can create custom themed elements by keeping the core properties and reduce the divergence among UI units. You won't overwrite but extend.

Suit also includes Grunt tasks for SASS lint and CSS minify configured app-wisely. Therefore you can build up your pages based on the element requirements. For instance the group named datasheet compiled for this CV site uses only a fragment (2KB gzipped) of the styleguide app (yet 12KB).

However it's pretty far from production ready you can watch Suit evolving on Github.

Snorky

Ustream, 2010 — Present

Named after the funkiest member of The Banana Splits, Snorky is our cross product UI module collection for several Ustream projects from the customized Zendesk-based Support Center to Ustream Demand's feature listings.

As we began to separate several side-projects from the monolithic code of Ustream.tv there was a demand for partly customizable stand-alone building bricks and widgets so I decided to develop and store them in an own repository where anyone can access, maintain and extend them.

After a year or so we have dozens of modules, plugins, extensions and even entire apps like this video slider which combines the seek bar module with the buttons extended with icons.

Ustream Pro

Ustream, 2010 — Present

From the upfront pricing page to the last submit button of Ustream's flagship product the recurring Pro Broadcasting platform, our job was to make the subscription flow as easy and hassle-free as possible. This was my first project at Ustream where the two front end developers, the UX expert and the owner of the product worked together from the very beginning. Starting with the first prototypes and user stories finishing with documentation and CasperJS tests.

Here's a snapshot of my favourite part of the sign up form. We call it smart credit card module. It selects your card type based on the entered number, creates a hint for your CVV field according to the card type and also prepares a familiar preview panel for the overview. (This 2 step version of the purchase flow is only available for Japanese locale, that's why you see the Kanjis.)

Ustream Ad Free

Ustream, 2010 — Present

The small brother of Pro Broadcasting is Ad Free with a 3-phase landing page, based on the client's browser resolution. It also has an integrated sign up form (which is only available for registered users) and the above mentioned smart credit card module.

If you look at my mobile view snapshot carefully you can notice a Minion and his button in the upper right corner. This is a treat for the fellow QA workers from one of my mates and me. Based on a PHP get param he appears and if you focus into any of the inputs on the form and press the button it fills out the entire form based on the field types and requirements.

The Ad Free and Pro landing pages both use the illustrative icon module developed for my Snorky project.

Network.hu

Network.hu, 2007 — 2009

At the dawn of social media Network.hu was one of the first community sites in Hungary besides the since deceased iWiW. As the half party of the front end developer team beyond building & maintaining the entire platform's UI, my favourite part was to come up with an easily customizable design layer to the so called club pages: [something].network.hu. In cooperation with the designer guy we came up with a strict rule based HTML + CSS package system for each type of content boxes from slide shows to polls and even link colors. Therefore you could easily apply a consistent and unique color scheme to your page. This was way before SASS and front end frameworks.
